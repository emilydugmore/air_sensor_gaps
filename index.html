Regional patterns

So why are there such big differences in air quality measures regionally?

The charts reveal stark contrasts in sensor density and data coverage across world regions — patterns that reflect much deeper structural inequalities in who gets to measure, and who gets measured.

Why the gaps?

The disparities in air quality monitoring are driven by a combination of factors. Select a theme to explore:

South Korea has the best sensor coverage in the world, with 88% of the population living within 5 km of an air quality sensor.

Just next door, North Korea has none. The border between these two countries represents one of the starkest data divides on Earth — driven not by geography, but by governance.

Chicago benefits from a dense, well-funded sensor network embedded across its neighbourhoods.

Cairo, one of the world's most air-polluted cities, has comparatively sparse monitoring infrastructure. What we can't see, we can't act on.

Regions in Africa are among the most populated in the world, but little air measurement data is available.

This is largely due to economic and infrastructure constraints — sensors are expensive to procure, maintain and calibrate.
